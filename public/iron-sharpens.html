Governing Principles

How we gather

1

“This group exists to build up each and every member.”

1 Corinthians 14:26 (ESV)

26 What then, brothers? When you come together, each one has a hymn, a lesson, a revelation, a tongue, or an interpretation. Let all things be done for building up.

This group exists to build up each and every member of the body of Christ.

2

“Each member has giftings of the Spirit and we must exercise those gifts for the common good.”

1 Corinthians 12:1–11 (ESV)

1 Now concerning spiritual gifts, brothers, I do not want you to be uninformed. 2 You know that when you were pagans you were led astray to mute idols, however you were led. 3 Therefore I want you to understand that no one speaking in the Spirit of God ever says “Jesus is accursed!” and no one can say “Jesus is Lord” except in the Holy Spirit. 4 Now there are varieties of gifts, but the same Spirit; 5 and there are varieties of service, but the same Lord; 6 and there are varieties of activities, but it is the same God who empowers them all in everyone. 7 To each is given the manifestation of the Spirit for the common good. 8 For to one is given through the Spirit the utterance of wisdom, and to another the utterance of knowledge according to the same Spirit, 9 to another faith by the same Spirit, to another gifts of healing by the one Spirit, 10 to another the working of miracles, to another prophecy, to another the ability to distinguish between spirits, to another various kinds of tongues, to another the interpretation of tongues. 11 All these are empowered by one and the same Spirit, who apportions to each one individually as he wills.

Each member has been given a manifestation of the Spirit for the common good. We exist to help each member know what that manifestation is.

3

“We will give greater honor to the less honorable. Outdo each other with honor.”

1 Corinthians 12:12–26 (ESV)

12 For just as the body is one and has many members, and all the members of the body, though many, are one body, so it is with Christ. 13 For in one Spirit we were all baptized into one body—Jews or Greeks, slaves or free—and all were made to drink of one Spirit. 14 For the body does not consist of one member but of many. 15 If the foot should say, “Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body,” that would not make it any less a part of the body. 16 And if the ear should say, “Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body,” that would not make it any less a part of the body. 17 If the whole body were an eye, where would be the sense of hearing? If the whole body were an ear, where would be the sense of smell? 18 But as it is, God arranged the members in the body, each one of them, as he chose. 19 If all were a single member, where would the body be? 20 As it is, there are many parts, yet one body. 21 The eye cannot say to the hand, “I have no need of you,” nor again the head to the feet, “I have no need of you.” 22 On the contrary, the parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, 23 and on those parts of the body that we think less honorable we bestow the greater honor, and our unpresentable parts are treated with greater modesty, 24 which our more presentable parts do not require. But God has so composed the body, giving greater honor to the part that lacked it, 25 that there may be no division in the body, but that the members may have the same care for one another. 26 If one member suffers, all suffer together; if one member is honored, all rejoice together.

God has put the body together, giving greater honor to the less honorable, so that there would be no division, but that the members would have the same concern for each other.

4

“This group is an ekklēsia and we should know the who, what, why of our roles in the gathering.”

1 Corinthians 12:27–31 (ESV)

27 Now you are the body of Christ and individually members of it. 28 And God has appointed in the church first apostles, second prophets, third teachers, then miracles, then gifts of healing, helping, administrating, and various kinds of tongues. 29 Are all apostles? Are all prophets? Are all teachers? Do all work miracles? 30 Do all possess gifts of healing? Do all speak with tongues? Do all interpret? 31 But earnestly desire the higher gifts. And I will show you a still more excellent way.

This group is an ekklēsia, a gathering of members of the body of Christ. God has appointed various functions in the church. We should be wise to pursue and understand those roles.

5

“We will not be noisy gongs or clanging cymbals.”

1 Corinthians 13:1–13 (ESV)

1 If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am a noisy gong or a clanging cymbal. 2 And if I have prophetic powers, and understand all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have all faith, so as to remove mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. 3 If I give away all I have, and if I deliver up my body to be burned, but have not love, I gain nothing. 4 Love is patient and kind; love does not envy or boast; it is not arrogant 5 or rude. It does not insist on its own way; it is not irritable or resentful; 6 it does not rejoice at wrongdoing, but rejoices with the truth. 7 Love bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things. 8 Love never ends. As for prophecies, they will pass away; as for tongues, they will cease; as for knowledge, it will pass away. 9 For we know in part and we prophesy in part, 10 but when the perfect comes, the partial will pass away. 11 When I was a child, I spoke like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I gave up childish ways. 12 For now we see in a mirror dimly, but then face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I have been fully known. 13 So now faith, hope, and love abide, these three; but the greatest of these is love.

We will not be noisy gongs or clanging cymbals. Every action should be a manifestation of genuine love for our brothers.

6

“Less is more. Ask questions before teaching. Let the Holy Spirit teach.”

1 Corinthians 14:1–25 (ESV)

1 Pursue love, and earnestly desire the spiritual gifts, especially that you may prophesy. 2 For one who speaks in a tongue speaks not to men but to God; for no one understands him, but he utters mysteries in the Spirit. 3 On the other hand, the one who prophesies speaks to people for their upbuilding and encouragement and consolation. 4 The one who speaks in a tongue builds up himself, but the one who prophesies builds up the church. 5 Now I want you all to speak in tongues, but even more to prophesy. The one who prophesies is greater than the one who speaks in tongues, unless someone interprets, so that the church may be built up. 6 Now, brothers, if I come to you speaking in tongues, how will I benefit you unless I bring you some revelation or knowledge or prophecy or teaching? 7 If even lifeless instruments, such as the flute or the harp, do not give distinct notes, how will anyone know what is played? 8 And if the bugle gives an indistinct sound, who will get ready for battle? 9 So with yourselves, if with your tongue you utter speech that is not intelligible, how will anyone know what is said? For you will be speaking into the air. 10 There are doubtless many different languages in the world, and none is without meaning, 11 but if I do not know the meaning of the language, I will be a foreigner to the speaker and the speaker a foreigner to me. 12 So with yourselves, since you are eager for manifestations of the Spirit, strive to excel in building up the church. 13 Therefore, one who speaks in a tongue should pray that he may interpret. 14 For if I pray in a tongue, my spirit prays but my mind is unfruitful. 15 What am I to do? I will pray with my spirit, but I will pray with my mind also; I will sing praise with my spirit, but I will sing with my mind also. 16 Otherwise, if you give thanks with your spirit, how can anyone in the position of an outsider say “Amen” to your thanksgiving when he does not know what you are saying? 17 For you may be giving thanks well enough, but the other person is not being built up. 18 I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you. 19 Nevertheless, in church I would rather speak five words with my mind in order to instruct others, than ten thousand words in a tongue. 20 Brothers, do not be children in your thinking. Be infants in evil, but in your thinking be mature. 21 In the Law it is written, “By people of strange tongues and by the lips of foreigners will I speak to this people, and even then they will not listen to me, says the Lord.” 22 Thus tongues are a sign not for believers but for unbelievers, while prophecy is a sign not for unbelievers but for believers. 23 If, therefore, the whole church comes together and all speak in tongues, and outsiders or unbelievers enter, will they not say that you are out of your minds? 24 But if all prophesy, and an unbeliever or outsider enters, he is convicted by all, he is called to account by all, 25 the secrets of his heart are disclosed, and so, falling on his face, he will worship God and declare that God is really among you.

We will prioritize prophecy over speaking in other tongues. “For you may very well be giving thanks, but the other person is not being built up… I would rather speak 5 words with understanding… than 10,000 in another tongue.”

If you have to share a lengthy message to set up the context, ask the Lord if this is the time for that. It's better to ask a question in 5 words than to teach what the Spirit has revealed to you. Teaching takes 10,000 words. Asking a question takes much less.

Or are you the only ones it has reached? 37 If anyone thinks that he is a prophet, or spiritual, he should acknowledge that the things I am writing to you are a command of the Lord. 38 If anyone does not recognize this, he is not recognized. 39 So, my brothers, earnestly desire to prophesy, and do not forbid speaking in tongues. 40 All things should be done decently and in order.

Practical Reminders

Can be shared collectively or with certain individuals.

  • We are not here to be teachers, but students.
  • Seek the new revelation — what is the fresh manna for today?
  • Don't dwell on what others need to hear. Share what you are hearing — it may help someone else.
  • Don't overshare. Let truth speak, cut to the heart, leave room for the quiet.
  • Actively invite everyone to participate.

Mission & Method

When done decently and in order, with genuine love, exercising our various giftings with wisdom, we have all walked away deeply encouraged and edified by the Holy Spirit with increased faith, love and fellowship with our Father and his Son Jesus Christ and with each other. When not done decently and in order, when we fall short of these writings, many of us walk away wondering if this ekklēsia is profitable and therefore even worth attending.

The mission of this group is to function as a mini ekklēsia of Christ. It looks exactly like what is outlined in 1 Corinthians 14:26–40. Instead of a traditional preacher/teacher standing up and lecturing, we will collectively model how to effectively and rightly divide the word of truth. The text and the original author is the preacher/teacher. We typically use a form of the SOAP process. The leader(s) are challenged to model effective methods and tools for reading, studying, and rightly dividing scripture.

We typically read a section of scripture and then re-read and meditate on those precious words of truth as guided by the Holy Spirit to more fully understand the original intent of the author who was guided by the Holy Spirit. We are mining for gold. Many don't realize the riches to be unearthed in scripture. As we dig together, several have seen the light and are motivated to dig on their own. The Holy Spirit may lead your individual thoughts and meditations to other gold deposits. Although this other deposit may be essential and foundational golden nuggets of precious life-giving truth, we ask that you not disrupt the mining and unearthing of the precious life-giving truths that we are discussing if it is not directly relevant to the message and intent of the original author. We ask that our elders in the faith help the youngers in the faith. Participate in teaching them how to dig where we are. Ask great questions. Challenge current understanding with thought-provoking questions. Let's stop digging for our brothers and help them learn how to dig.

As we dig and observe and reveal truth, we ask the Lord to reveal what that truth means to the body and to me personally. Prayer is a pivotal engagement throughout the process:

  • Lord, where should I dig?
  • Lord, how shall I dig?
  • Lord, what have I just dug up?
  • Lord, this looks amazing — what is its value?
  • Lord, I am convicted — what should I do with this?
  • Lord, you have revealed amazing truth — what should I do with it?

If you are digging in preparation for our gathering, and the Lord leads you to share a foundational faith lesson outside the direct context of our reading, please consult with the group leader prior to the gathering.

Iron Sharpens — Logos Group

2-Hour Facilitator Guide

Governing Principles

  • We are not here to be teachers, but students.
  • Seek the new revelation — what is the fresh manna for today?
  • Don't dwell on what others need to hear. Share what you are hearing — it may help someone else.
  • Don't overshare. Let truth speak, cut to the heart, leave room for the quiet.
  • Actively invite everyone to participate.

Session Flow

1. Fellowship & Grace for Late Arrivals

15 minutes
  • Welcome everyone as they arrive. Keep the atmosphere warm and unhurried — this is not dead time, it is relational glue.
  • Light conversation only. Avoid diving into the topic early — save energy for the group.
  • Hold space for latecomers without drawing attention to their arrival.

2. Invite Father, Son & Holy Spirit

5 minutes
  • Open with a brief, sincere prayer — not a lecture, just an invitation. Ask the Holy Spirit to be the teacher in the room.
  • Briefly remind the group of the governing principles, especially: “We are here as students, not teachers.”

    If you feel a calling to teach for lengthy periods of time, you are invited to start a community group with that in mind.

3. Personal Ministry & Prayer

5–35 minutes

This is a whole-group time, not pairs. The cycle for each person:

Person shares need → Group ministers → Group prays specifically
  • Open the floor to the whole group. Invite anyone to share a personal need, situation, or burden they'd like the group to minister to.
  • Once someone shares, create space for the group to respond — words of encouragement, Scripture, prophetic impressions, or simply presence. This is ministry, not advice-giving.
  • After the group has ministered, close each person's time with targeted, specific prayer — not general. Pray into exactly what was shared and what the Spirit surfaced.
  • Then open the floor again: “Who else would like the group to pray for them?” Repeat the cycle for each person who comes forward.
  • As facilitator, protect the pace. If one situation is drawing a long discussion, gently redirect: “Let's bring this to prayer now.” Ministry is not counseling.
  • This block is flexible by design. If the Spirit is moving deeply, honor that and compress the discussion time — encounter comes first.
The Mercy Seat
When sin or shame surfaces

When the Spirit exposes sin, shame, or accusation in a brother, walk him through these five movements. Don't rush — let each step be honest and complete before moving to the next.

  1. 1 Confession — Name the sin honestly before God and the brothers. No softening, no generalities.
  2. 2 Repentance — Turn from it. A deliberate change of mind and direction, agreeing with God against the sin.
  3. 3 Receive Forgiveness — Through faith in the blood of Christ. Not earned, not delayed — received now.
  4. 4 Proclaim the Truth — Speak aloud your position in Christ. Forgiven, righteous, hidden in Him, no longer condemned.
  5. 5 Rejoice as a Son of God — Step out of the shadow. Celebrate the Father's welcome — the brothers rejoice with him.

4. First Read-Through of the Passage

15 minutes
  • Distribute or display the passage. Read it aloud together — one voice or rotating voices, whatever serves the group best.
  • Instruct everyone to simply listen on this first pass. No notes, no commentary yet. Just receive.
  • After the reading, allow 30–60 seconds of silence. Let the text settle before moving on.

5. Deep Dive — Re-Read & Discussion

Remaining Time
  • Re-read the passage slowly and deliberately, pausing at natural breaks for reflection.
  • Open discussion using the guiding questions below. You don't need every question — let conversation breathe.
  • Resist filling silences too quickly. Silence invites the quiet voices in the room.
  • Gently redirect if someone becomes a teacher or monopolizes: “That's great — what's everyone else hearing?”
  • Watch the clock loosely — leave 5 minutes for closing prayer.
Guiding Questions

A toolkit for word-by-word, verse-by-verse digging. Pull from any section as the Spirit prompts — you don't need every question.

  • Which single word keeps drawing your eye? Why this one?
  • Are any words repeated? (Repetition = emphasis.)
  • Does the original Hebrew or Greek open up something the English flattens?
  • Is there a word here used rarely elsewhere in Scripture?
  • What verb tense or mood is used — command, promise, ongoing action, completed action?
  • Who do the pronouns refer to? ("He," "they," "you" — singular or plural?)
  • Are there names or titles of God here? Why this name and not another?
  • What connector words are present — therefore, but, so that, because, if/then, for? What do they link?
  • Is there a contrast (but, yet, however)?
  • Is there a comparison (like, as, so)?
  • Is there a cause and effect (therefore, because, so)?
  • Is there a list, progression, or pattern?
  • Is there a command, promise, warning, or principle in this verse?
  • What is the literary form — narrative, poetry, parable, prophecy, letter, law? How should that shape how we read it?
  • What happens in the verses immediately before and after?
  • What is the flow of the chapter — and where does this verse sit in it?
  • What is the purpose of the whole book, and how does this verse serve it?
  • What was happening historically and culturally when this was written?
  • Who is the author, and what do we know of his circumstances?
  • Who is the original audience, and what burden was he addressing?
  • What would the first hearers have understood that we might miss?
  • Does this passage echo or quote something earlier in Scripture?
  • Is this passage quoted, fulfilled, or developed later in Scripture?
  • How does this passage point to, prefigure, or reveal Jesus Christ?
  • How does this fit into the whole story of redemption — creation, fall, redemption, restoration?
  • What is the one main thing the author is trying to get across?
  • What is the author assuming the reader already knows or believes?
  • What is the author not saying that we might be tempted to add?
  • If we removed this verse, what would be lost from the chapter?
  • What does this say about God's character or heart?
  • What does this say about the human condition?
  • What does this say about the Kingdom?
  • What is fresh manna for you today in this verse?
  • What word or phrase stopped you as we read?
  • Where do you feel conviction, comfort, or wonder?
  • Is there a sin to confess?
  • Is there a promise to lay hold of?
  • Is there a command to obey?
  • Is there an example to follow — or avoid?
  • Is there a prayer the Spirit is forming in you right now?
  • What is one step you'll take this week because of this verse?
  • Who else needs to hear what the Lord just spoke to you?
  • "Say more about that — what in the text led you there?"
  • "What's everyone else hearing?"
  • "Can we hold that thought and stay in this verse a moment longer?"
  • "What word in this verse made you say that?"
  • "Who hasn't shared yet — what are you noticing?"
  • (silence — let it breathe)

(Add other leading questions as the Spirit prompts.)

6. Closing Prayer

5 minutes
  • Invite brief, voluntary prayers of response — thanksgiving, intercession, or declaration — from anyone in the group.
  • Close with a short prayer of gratitude, sealing what the Spirit did in the room. Keep it simple and genuine.
